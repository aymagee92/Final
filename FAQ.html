Taxation

  • What Forms Do I Need To File?

    The required forms vary based on alot of different factors. For individuals, most are required to file Form 1040 and a similar Form for the state they live in. There are various attachments and schedules that are also required to be filed. For 1099 recipients and single member LLC's are required to report on Schedule C of their individual return. For other business entities, they are required to file their own business returns, along with various attachments, and might also be required to issue Schedule K-1's to each owner. It takes years of training and the use of expensive softwares to get it right. One of the advantages in hiring professionals is that we have both.

  • What Forms Do I Need To Upload?

    For individual tax return prepration, we'll need (1) A copy of your prior year federal and state returns, (2) A copy of the driver's license for both taxpayer and spouse, (3) A copy of the social security cards for taxpayer, spouse, and all dependents on the return, and (4) the filled out tax organizer along with all of your tax documents for the year. For businesses, we'll reach out to you with a detailed list.

  • Will You Look Over My Prior Year Returns For Free?

    Oftentimes, taxpayers are not sure as to the quality of work done by their tax preparer. The best way to figure this out is to have another set of eyes review the return. We offer that free of charge to anyone who wants it. We will look over the return and let you know what we find. Please contact us to get started.

  • Are You Required To Report Errors To The IRS?

    No. If we find an error on the return, we are only required to inform the taxpayer of the error and the potential financial penalties of not correcting the error. We will not report the error to any agency. However, we do recommend fixing the error to avoid financial penalties and interest, and can assist you with that process if you desire.

  • How Many Years Back Can I Request A Refund?

    Generally, the statute of limitations for requesting a refund from the IRS is the later of either 3 years after the original due date, or 2 years after filing. This essentially means 3 years after the original due date, unless you file the return over a year late, in which case it will be 2 years from the date you first file the return.

  • Where Is My Refund?

    You can check with the various taxing authorities using the links above under the "Refund Trackers" tab. The government is required to pay you interest on the refund if it takes over 45 days to be issued. Please note that even though the agencies will direct you to check with your tax preparer, we cannot control the speed in which the taxing authorities process the returns. We are only able to confirm that the returns were properly submitted to them.

  • Can I Get A Refund Advance?

    We do not do any refund advances. We will always direct the government agencies to deposit any refund directly to your specified bank account.

  • What is an Extension?

    An extension extends the filing due date of the tax return, typically an additional six months from the original due date. However, this does not extend the deadline to pay any tax due on the due date. Therefore, if we file an extension for you, we will use the information you have provided up to that point to estimate how much you will owe.

  • How Do I Pay The Tax Owed?

    When we file your return or extension, we will request your bank routing number and account number so that we can schedule the tax to be paid at the date you specify. If you need to make an estimated tax payment or some other tax payment, you should go to irs.gov/payments to make the payment online. Make sure to print and save the payment confirmation number for your records. Many state revenue departments have similar online processes.

  • What Are The Penalties For Not Paying The Tax Owed?

    There are various different penalties and sub-scenerios within those penalties which makes exact calculation impossible. The IRS and state agencies rely on advanced computer softwares to calculate these amounts. If you'd like to estimate the potential penalties, we recommend going to irscalculators.com/irs-penalty-calculator. If you have years of unfiled taxes or a large amount due, we can help guide you through the process and apply for programs that can give you more time to pay and/or reduce the amount owed.

  • Can I Go To Jail If I Make An Unintentional Error On My Return?

    No. The IRS and state revenue departments have a structured process for informing taxpayers of errors they detect on a return. When an error is detected, they mail a letter notifying the taxpayer of the error. The taxpayer can then choose to either fix the return through filing an amended return, or go through an appeal process through the Independent Office of Appeals if they disagree. An Enrolled Agent can help you through the process.

  • What Is A Form W-4?

    A Form W-4 is used by W-2 employees to tell their employer how much the employer should withhold for Federal income tax. There is a similar form for states with income tax. The employer is required to give this to you when you start work. The form is confusing and frequently leads to people having to file returns to get their withholding back. We can help determine whether withholding is necessary for you, and if so, how to fill out the forms to request the appropriate amounts.

  • What Is A Healthcare Application?

    The federal government provides a healthcare program (frequently referred to as "Obamacare") that allows individuals without healthcare to sign up for a plan and receive payment assistance from the federal government. This application is deeply connected with your tax return. Anyone can apply, and you have the ability to choose not to sign up for a plan if you don't get approved for enough assistance. We can help you in filing the application and determining whether you are eligible for assistance and, if so, how much you would receive. Please also note that while you may not be required to pay any tax, receiving assitance for Obamacare creates a federal filing requirement at the end of the year. Due to the complex nature of the law and the tax implications it has, we recommend working with us when trying to apply.

  • I received a notice requesting I file Form 8962. What is that?

    Form 8962 is the form number used to reconcile the healthcare assitance. When the healthcare application is filed, you estimate the amount of income that you anticipate you will make for the year. The government then determines your assistance based on this estimate. The assitance will be paid monthly directly to the healthcare company every month to help pay for the monthly health insurance premium. This amount may pay for some or all of the premium, depending on how much you are approved for and/or how much you choose to use. At the end of the year, you must reconcile these assistance payments with the amount you were actually entitled to received based on your final income amount. This could lead to either receiving additional assitance or paying back some of the assistance. If you have received this notice, we can help.

  • What is an Enrolled Agent?

    Despite what the name insinuates, an Enrolled Agent has no affiliation with the IRS. It is one of three designations that the IRS recognizes as sufficient to fully represent taxpayers before the IRS. In order to receive the designation, the tax preparer must undergo an intense three part tax examination covering individual tax laws, business tax laws, and the rules for representing clients before the IRS. They also must take continuing education courses related to taxation ever year.

  • How does that compare with a CPA or a Lawyer?

    These designations are issued by states rather than the IRS. They allow the worker to charge for certain other services that are unrelated to tax work. As such, this means that they charge higher prices for their services and might not be keeping up with the changing tax laws. In fact, some of these individuals spend their whole careers never seeing a tax return.

Bookkeeping

  • What Software Do You Use?

    We will set you up in Quickbooks Online. We will guide you through how to download the app, which will allow you to always have your books right at your fingertips. If you choose to do it yourself, it will also allow you to categorize income and expenses with a simple swipe of your finger.

  • What Does "The Books" Mean?

    This term generally refers to your Balance Sheet and Income Statement. The Balance Sheet lists all your assets/v2 (what the business owns) and liabilities (what the business owes) as of any given date. The Income Statement lists all of your income (what the company made) and expenses (what the company paid) for the year. These are needed when you go to file your tax return every year.

  • What is Depreciation?

    Depreciation represents the cost of expensive assets/v2 that are purchased by the business. Calculating the correct depreciation figures is convoluted and varies based on numerous factors, including the type of entity, the type of the asset, and how the business chooses to keep their books. Keeping accurate and up-to-date depreciation records is vital for any business. Calculating depreciation is standard for any bookkeeping or tax work done, but we can also assist in calculating these values a la carte.

  • What Is Reconciling a Bank Account or Credit Card?

    This is the process of taking either a bank or credit card statement, and making sure all of the transactions are recorded on the books. The frequency depends on the size of the business, but should be done at least once a quarter.

Investment Management

  • What Software Do You Use?

    We use TD Ameritrade's ThinkOrSwim and Interactive Broker's Trader Workstation for trading. If you choose to do your own trading, we have a number of different tools for these platforms available for purchase in our store.

  • What Does Hedging Mean?

    Hedging means opening a position that has a negative correlation with your existing position. While this may seem counter-intuitive, hedging is a great way to reduce the risk of your investment and lock in small gains.

  • What Are Options?

    Options are contracts that obligate one party to sell 100 shares of stock to another party, at a specified price (referred to as the strike price), on a specific date (referred to as the expiration date). Since the contract involves exchanging stock, it is also referred to as a stock derivative because it derives it's value from the stock. There are three aspects that combine to form the option price: (1) the stock price, (2) the time until the option expires, and (3) the market volatility.

  • What Does Short Selling Mean?

    Selling short is the act of borrowing an asset from someone and selling it into the open market. When you sell something that is borrowed, you have to eventually buy it back from the open market and return it. When an investor holds a belief that the value of the asset will go down in the future and is correct, he will profit off the difference between what he sold it for and what he re-purchased it for, less the interest he pays to the borrowee.

  • Is Selling An Option The Same As Shorting?

    No. When you sell an option contract (also referred to as writing an option), you are essentially creating the contract and placing it for sale on the open market. There is no limit to the amount of contracts that can be created, and therefore, there is never a need to borrow. On the contrary, stock has a fixed amount of shares issued by the company at a given point in time and new shares cannot be created by the seller. Therefore, if the seller wishes to sell shares but doesn't own any, he must borrow from someone who does.
